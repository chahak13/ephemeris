Problem Statement

  • To determine the residence-time matrix for a city, we need to estimate the mobility of individuals. We use Brownian Bridges to model this movement of individuals.
  • Horne et al. discuss the usage of Brownian bridges to model the movement of animals in forest in their paper Analyzing animal movements using Brownian Bridges. We follow their approach to do the same for individuals in a city.

Brownian Bridge

  • Let the position of an individual at time \(t_1\) be \(z_1\) and at time \(t_2\) be \(z_2\). Then, its path of movement to go from \(z_1\) to \(z_2\) can be modelled as a random walk.
  • If a Brownian motion is assumed between these two points, then \(z_t\) - the position of the individual at time \(t \in [t_1, t_2]\), is distributed according to a bivariate distribution with mean and covariance matrix given by

\[

\begin{align*} \mu(t; z_1, z_2) &= z_1 + (z_2 - z_1)\alpha \\ \Sigma(t; \sigma) &= \sigma_m^2 \left[\frac{(t - t_1)(t_2 - t)}{(t_2 - t_1)} \right]\mathbb{I}\\ \end{align*}

\]

where \(\alpha = (t - t_1)/(t_2 - t_1)\), \(\mathbb{I}\) is the 2x2 identity matrix and \(\sigma_m^2\) is the Brownian motion variance related to the mobility.

  • Such a motion has variance 0 when \(t = t_1\) or \(t = t_2\) and the maximum variance at the centre of the straight line joining the two points. This motion is called a Brownian Bridge.

Estimating probability of a point in space

  • Theoretically, the variance at the endpoints of a bridge is 0, but practically, that may not be the case. To accomodate that, we assume that even the endpoints are bivariate normal distributions, i.e. \(N(z_1, \delta^2\mathbb{I})\) and \(N(z_2, \delta^2\mathbb{I})\), where \(\delta^2\) is the variance of the location error.

  • Now, we can calculate the marginal probability density at position \(z\) at time \(t \in [t_1, t_2]\) as

    \[

    \begin{equation} f\left(z;t,\sigma_{m},\delta\right) = \int\int\phi\left(z;\mu(t;a,b),\Sigma(t;\sigma_{m})\right)\,\phi\left(a;z_{1},\delta^2\mathbb{I}\right)\phi\left(b;z_{2}, \delta^2\mathbb{I}\right)da\,db, \nonumber \label{MarginalDensity01} \end{equation}

    \]

  • Therefore, we can calculate the probability density at position \(z\) by

    \[

    \begin{align} h\left(z;\sigma_{m},\delta\right) &= \frac{1}{(t_{2}-t_{1})}\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}f\left(z;t,\sigma_{m},\delta\right)dt \nonumber \\ &= \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}\phi\left(z;\mu(t),\sigma(t;\sigma_{m},\delta)\mathbb{I}\right)dt, \label{MarginalDensity02} \end{align}

    \]

    where \(T = t_2 - t_1\), \(\mu(t) = z_1 + (z_2 - z_1)\alpha\), \(\sigma(t; \sigma_m, \delta) = T\alpha (1 - \alpha)\sigma_m^2 + [(1-\alpha)^2 + \alpha^2]\delta^2\) and \(\alpha = t/T\).

Brownian bridges for all individuals

  • We have GPS data points for ~3 million Hermosillo residents over a few months. Using this data, we can generate Brownian Bridges motion for each individual between two points in time.
  • We can use these brownian motion to estimate the marginal probability density at a point, with the resulting probability density being the sum of computations for all the brownian bridges.
  • Once we have the probability densities at each point, we can calculate the cumulative probability density of an AGEB (locality) by adding the values for each point in the AGEB.
